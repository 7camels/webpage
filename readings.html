Readings

“In Plato’s Cave” from On Photography by Susan Sontag

Photography to me, has always been one of my primary instruments of self-expression. Having the ability to capture a moment in time has always felt both a responsibility and a gift. Whether it be through a smartphone, DSLR, or a point-and-shoot, I love photographing what catches my eye. I take large interest in the visual geometry of the world and would say that it’s visible in the photos I take. The versatility of the photograph is something always present on my mind as well; from lighthearted candids of loved ones to intentionally resonant stills. Sontag states that photographs “alter and enlarge our notions of what is worth looking at” and calls them “an ethics of seeing.” That brings me back to the responsibility of photography—where I understand the importance of a visual curation. Where “seeing comes before words,” the assumed value of a photo reflects the photographer’s own taste and artistic identity. So when I shoot something, I know and feel that it is something of value to me.

In my own artistic journey, I have grown positively to a state that feels less like performance and more as an exhibiting of my own taste and ideas. A younger me definitely grappled with the “necessity” to record/archive everything. While I agree with Sontag’s take that photographs can reduce experiences into “souvenirs,” I point back to the responsibility of the photographer. And towing that line between capturing a moment or extracting from it, I believe there is art of great value to be achieved. Now more than ever, do I feel as present when I take pictures of what is visually appealing to me. When the prime motive is to tell a story with each photo rather than add to a feed, each take feels more artistically definite.

The importance of photography is also something to be considered as we enter this new “surveil-state” craze. Does everything need to be photographed and/or documented? To me, there is beauty in the ephemeral, where moments can be forgotten but still carry weight in our lives. It feels indirectly human; to forget. Before advancements in technology, we carried those moments in our hearts and minds where I believe they are still most powerful. That being said, Sontag attributes photography as a tool of surveillance and control, to which I agree with. Again, the responsibility of the photographer. To truly represent the world at its current state, I believe it would be through motifs of the human struggle against surveillance.

I’m sure the constant feeling of being watched was felt even by our primordial ancestors. But to the degree of present-day society (at least in the US), is something relatively new. The camera at first was novel, then it had shook up the art world replacing painting (in many ways but admittedly not all), but now it feels so entrenched in our infrastructure. Image-making will always uphold the hierarchy of the photographer and the photographed; to truly respect the visual medium, one must maintain their identity and taste in order to make images that are honest about the world they come from.
